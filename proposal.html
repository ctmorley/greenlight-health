Why This Niche?

The Burden

  • Diagnostic radiology: 91% of encounters require PA
  • Radiation oncology: 97%
  • Cardiology: 93%
  • Neurosurgery: 90%
  • Imaging denial rates: 25-30% vs 7.7% overall MA average

The Complexity

  • Multiple RBMs (EviCore, Carelon, NIA) with different portals and rules
  • Payers switch RBMs without notice
  • Clinical criteria matching (ACR, MCG, InterQual)
  • PACS/RIS integration requirements
  • Multi-step procedure coordination (biopsy after imaging)
  • Site-of-service optimization

Clinical Data Strategy: Payer Rules & Criteria Sources

The clinical rules engine is GreenLight's core IP. This section maps the authoritative data sources that power payer-specific PA determination, denial prevention, and appeal generation.

Tiered Data Acquisition Strategy

Tier 1 — Public Clinical Standards (Free)

Foundational clinical correctness layer. Available immediately, no licensing required.

  • ACR Appropriateness Criteria: ~200+ clinical conditions mapped to imaging modalities. Each topic rated 1–9 (usually appropriate / may be appropriate / usually not appropriate). Machine-readable via ACR API at acsearch.acr.org
  • CMS NCDs & LCDs: National & Local Coverage Determinations define exactly what Medicare covers and what requires PA. Published in structured formats at cms.gov
  • CMS Annual PA List: Medicare Advantage prior authorization requirements published annually — definitive source for MA plans
No costImmediate accessMachine-readable

Tier 2 — RBM Published Guidelines (Free)

The actual criteria that determine approval/denial for most commercial plans. Published as PDFs, parseable with AI.

  • EviCore (Evernorth/Cigna): Clinical guidelines at evicore.com/provider, organized by specialty — radiology, cardiology, oncology, musculoskeletal
  • Carelon (Elevance/Anthem): Clinical appropriateness guidelines at carelonmedicalbenefits.com — covers imaging, radiation therapy, sleep studies
  • NIA / Evolent Health: National Imaging Associates guidelines on provider portal — primarily advanced imaging (MRI, CT, PET, nuclear medicine)
No costPDF parsing requiredHighest denial-prevention value

Tier 3 — Payer-Specific Policies (Free, Manual)

Individual payer PA requirements by CPT code, plan type, and state. Scattered across provider portals.

  • UnitedHealthcare: uhcprovider.com → Prior Authorization & Notification lookup tool
  • Aetna: aetna.com/health-care-professionals → Precertification lists by plan
  • Cigna: cignaforhcp.cigna.com → Coverage Policies searchable by CPT
  • BCBS: Varies by state plan — each publishes a medical policy manual with PA requirements
  • Humana, Anthem, Centene: Provider portals with PA lookup tools
No costHigh maintenanceChanges without notice

Tier 4 — Licensed Clinical Criteria (Commercial)

Industry-standard clinical decision support used by payers themselves to adjudicate PA requests.

  • InterQual (Cotiviti): Industry-standard criteria used by major payers. Structured, machine-readable criteria sets. Covers imaging appropriateness, surgical necessity, and level-of-care
  • MCG (Milliman Care Guidelines): Used by ~2,700 hospitals and health plans. Covers imaging, surgical, and procedure appropriateness with evidence-based indications
Commercial licenseAuthoritativeMachine-readable

Why This Is the Moat

The rules engine is the product. The platform UI and workflow automation are table stakes. The real value is knowing — for a specific payer, plan, state, CPT code, and clinical scenario — exactly what documentation is needed, what criteria must be met, and what language will get an approval. This knowledge compound over time as GreenLight processes more PAs and learns from outcomes.
Keeping rules current is the moat. Payers change PA requirements frequently and often without notice. EviCore updates clinical guidelines semi-annually. State Medicaid programs change quarterly. Building a system that continuously monitors, parses, and ingests these changes — before they cause denials — is what separates GreenLight from horizontal PA tools.

APPENDIX

Platform Naming Considerations

Five naming options evaluated for market positioning, memorability, and brand extensibility.

1. GreenLight by Medivis

"Get the green light for every authorization."
RECOMMENDED
Pros: Intuitive metaphor — "green light" = approval. Works as a verb ("GreenLight your MRI"). Memorable and distinctive. Strong brand color opportunity.
Risks: May feel too casual for enterprise healthcare. Common phrase could present trademark challenges.

2. ClearPath Health

"Clearing the path through authorization."
Pros: Professional, implies efficiency and obstacle removal. Easy to understand. Works well for enterprise sales.
Risks: Generic sounding. Multiple companies use "ClearPath" across industries. Less distinctive.

3. Aperture Health

"Precision focus on authorization."
Pros: Aperture = opening in optics/imaging. Perfect imaging reference. Sophisticated and unique. Strong visual brand potential.
Risks: Portal video game association (Aperture Science). Not immediately obvious meaning to non-technical audience.

4. PriorIQ

"Intelligent prior authorization."
Pros: Tech-forward, category-defining. Immediately communicates function. "IQ" implies intelligence/AI. Compact and memorable.
Risks: Narrow — sounds like it only does PA (which is the intent, but limits future expansion). May feel cold.

5. SwiftAuth

"Authorization at the speed of care."
Pros: Speed + authorization. Direct and action-oriented. Easy to remember. Clean URL availability likely.
Risks: Sounds like a cybersecurity/authentication company (OAuth, AuthO). May confuse the market.
Recommendation: GreenLight by Medivis is the strongest choice. The "green light" metaphor is universally understood, emotionally positive, and directly maps to the core value proposition — getting authorization approvals. The "by Medivis" parent brand adds enterprise credibility and positions the platform within a broader healthcare technology portfolio. It works as both a noun ("the GreenLight platform") and a verb ("GreenLight your imaging orders"), giving it natural virality in clinical conversations.
